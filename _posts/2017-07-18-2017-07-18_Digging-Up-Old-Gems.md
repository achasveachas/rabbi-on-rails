<h3 name="3c32" id="3c32" class="graf graf--h3 graf--leading graf--title">Digging Up Old Gems</h3>
<figure name="11e0" id="11e0" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--h3"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*Kk1bEOjNn4bYxxC164yjgg.jpeg" src="/assets/images/posts/2017-07-18-2017-07-18_Digging-Up-Old-Gems-0.jpeg"></figure><p name="091b" id="091b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Back when I was just starting at <a href="https://medium.com/u/973c5cbfb09b" data-href="https://medium.com/u/973c5cbfb09b" data-anchor-type="2" data-user-id="973c5cbfb09b" data-action-value="973c5cbfb09b" data-action="show-user-card" data-action-type="hover" class="markup--user markup--p-user" target="_blank">Flatiron School</a>, one of our first projects and the first major project was to make a Ruby gem that a user could interact with using the Command Line.</p>
<p name="9f94" id="9f94" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">At the time it was a major task, it put my fragile knowledge of Object Oriented Ruby to the test. I wrote about what it felt like in <a href="https://blog.yechiel.me/gem-install-swim-82ff7a95e605" data-href="https://blog.yechiel.me/gem-install-swim-82ff7a95e605" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">this </a>post.</p>
<p name="c0da" id="c0da" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The project was quite simple; I used Nokogiri to scrape a deals blog so that a user could see a synopsis of that day’s deals without leaving their terminal (and if you are so dedicated to your terminal that would find that remotely useful kudos to you :)</p>
<p name="96c1" id="96c1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">At the time I felt that was a nifty trick, I even published my gem for the world to enjoy on <a href="https://rubygems.org/gems/dansdeals" data-href="https://rubygems.org/gems/dansdeals" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">RubyGems</a>, and then promptly never touched it.</p>
<p name="d704" id="d704" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Recently I noticed that <a href="https://www.dansdeals.com/" data-href="https://www.dansdeals.com/" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">DansDeals</a>, the site I was scraping, went through an overhaul, and that’s when I discovered first-hand why scraping is unreliable; when I tried running the gem, it crashed immediately. None of the CSS selectors I so painstakingly taught Nokogiri to look out for where in the new layout, and my poor gem couldn’t find any deal.</p>
<p name="2ad1" id="2ad1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">My first impulse was to just forget about it; I have since moved on to bigger and better projects, this gem wasn’t even on my portfolio site anymore. It was nice while it lasted, but all nice things come to an end.</p>
<p name="338b" id="338b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">But then I figured why not give it a look? How hard would it be? There’s always the chance that the new layout would turn out to be a Nokogiri death-trap, but why not try at least? I figured I’d give it an hour tops, and if it weren’t fixed by then, I would leave it buried in the depths of my GitHub account.</p>
<p name="4433" id="4433" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So I opened up the site, fired up the console, and within a half hour <a href="https://rubygems.org/gems/dansdeals" data-href="https://rubygems.org/gems/dansdeals" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">dansdeals 2.0</a> was live on RubyGems.</p>

<script async src="https://platform.twitter.com/widgets.js" charset="utf-8"></script></figure><p name="b5f7" id="b5f7" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Why did I do it? It’s not like anyone would ever use this gem (including me), so why bother?</p>
<blockquote name="387a" id="387a" class="graf graf--pullquote graf-after--p">
<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Mallory" data-href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Mallory" class="markup--anchor markup--pullquote-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">George Mallory</a> was a climber who became famous for his attempts to scale the world’s tallest mountain, Mt. Everest (he sadly died during his third attempt in 1924, 25 years before Hillary and Tenzing finally made it to the summit). In a New York Times interview, he was asked “Why do you want to climb Mount Everest?” his answer was so simple it became world-famous: “because it’s there.”</blockquote>
<p name="2aef" id="2aef" class="graf graf--p graf-after--pullquote graf--trailing">I guess I just wanted to fix my old useless gem because I could.</p>
