<h3 name="e611" id="e611" class="graf graf--h3 graf--leading graf--title">A Tale Of Two Inaugurations</h3>
<figure name="9a6a" id="9a6a" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--h3"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*tPgYO6FDxzPBs58S.jpg" data-width="624" data-height="351" data-is-featured="true" alt="A picture of the inauguration of president Barack Obama." src="/assets/images/posts/2021-01-22-2021-01-22_A-Tale-Of-Two-Inaugurations-0.jpg"></figure><p name="2ad3" id="2ad3" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Wednesday, the United States and a large part of the world watched as the US swore in Joe Biden as their 46th president.</p>
<p name="0cd4" id="0cd4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In his inaugural address, President Biden spoke about unity:</p>
<blockquote name="5e75" id="5e75" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p">“To overcome these challenges, to restore the soul and secure the future of America requires so much more than words. It requires the most elusive of all things in a democracy: Unity.</blockquote>
<blockquote name="94fb" id="94fb" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--blockquote">“In another January, on New Year’s Day in 1863, Abraham Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation. When he put pen to paper, the president said, and I quote: “If my name ever goes down into history, it’ll be for this act. And my whole soul is in it.”</blockquote>
<blockquote name="9205" id="9205" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--blockquote">“My whole soul was in it today. On this January day, my whole soul is in this: Bringing America together, uniting our people, uniting our nation. And I ask every American to join me in this cause.</blockquote>
<blockquote name="2a41" id="2a41" class="graf graf--blockquote graf-after--blockquote">Uniting to fight the foes we face: anger, resentment, hatred, extremism, lawlessness, violence, disease, joblessness and hopelessness. With unity, we can do great things.”</blockquote>
<p name="728a" id="728a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--blockquote">There is no question that the political discourse has devolved into extreme divisiveness over the last few years, and it’s refreshing to hear from a leader who will do his best to heal the divide rather than fan its flames.</p>
<figure name="7411" id="7411" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="0*0jRKw-C9QmteZ0Bs.jpg" data-width="550" data-height="687" alt="A young picture of the Lubavitcher Rebbe." src="/assets/images/posts/2021-01-22-2021-01-22_A-Tale-Of-Two-Inaugurations-1.jpg"><figcaption class="imageCaption">The Lubavitcher Rebbe around the time he assumed leadership of the Chabad Lubavitch movement (1951)</figcaption></figure><p name="e4cf" id="e4cf" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">But what does unity even mean? Does it mean that everyone has to conform to the same ideas? Do we have to give up our individuality? Are we required to overlook hate and intolerance in the name of “unity”?</p>
<p name="98d1" id="98d1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Seventy years ago to the day, on the tenth of Shvat 5711 (Jan 17, 1951), a different inaugural address took place.</p>
<p name="ef83" id="ef83" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson assumed the leadership of the Chabad Lubavitch movement after the passing of his father in law, and in the first public talk he gave after accepting the position, he said the following:</p>
<blockquote name="28d2" id="28d2" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--p">“When my father-in-law, the Rebbe, arrived in America, he quoted the words of the Sages “When you come to a town, follow its customs.” Here in America, people like to hear a “mission statement,” a declaration that is novel and preferably sensational. I don’t know whether there is a need for things to be done in this way, but “when you come to a town, follow its customs.”</blockquote>
<blockquote name="9d35" id="9d35" class="graf graf--blockquote graf--startsWithDoubleQuote graf-after--blockquote">“The three loves — the love of G‑d, the love of the Torah, and love toward a fellow Jew — are all one. They are by definition indivisible, like one essence. If a person has a love of G‑d but is without a love of the Torah or love of his fellow Jew, this indicates that something is lacking in his love of G‑d, too. On the other hand, when there is <em class="markup--em markup--blockquote-em">Ahavas Yisrael,</em> then even though this is [merely one] <em class="markup--em markup--blockquote-em">mitzvah,</em> it ultimately leads to a love of the Torah and a love of G‑d.”</blockquote>
<p name="52df" id="52df" class="graf graf--p graf-after--blockquote">Real unity is around a shared goal and a shared purpose. In the context of Ahavat Yisrael (love of your fellow Jew), that shared purpose is Ahavat Hashem (love of G-d) and Ahavat Hatorah (love of the Torah).</p>
<p name="2fd6" id="2fd6" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">I believe that in the context of political unity, the shared purpose is a love of one’s country. When we acknowledge that, at the end of the day, we all want what’s best for the country, we can put aside personal and political differences and work together toward a common goal.</p>
<p name="e250" id="e250" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">But just as a person can’t have a true love of Hashem if they don’t love their fellow Jew, similarly, if we let patriotism and love of our country get in the way of our respect for others’ humanity and dignity, then our faux patriotism and love of country aren’t real. They’re merely a cover to justify our disdain for the “other.”</p>
<p name="3459" id="3459" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p graf--trailing">Let us take this moment to reflect, put aside our differences, and work together to build a world that is better for all of us.</p>
