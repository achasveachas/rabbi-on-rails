<h3 name="c5a5" id="c5a5" class="graf graf--h3 graf--leading graf--title">Welcome to the MVC Restaurant</h3>
<h4 name="5c45" id="5c45" class="graf graf--h4 graf-after--h3 graf--subtitle">A new look at an old analogy</h4>
<p name="53c2" id="53c2" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h4"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">I was recently asked to describe the common components of web application frameworks. What purpose does each component serve? What is the benefit of separating each component from the others?</em></p>
<p name="ab71" id="ab71" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">The most common architecture web frameworks follow is the MVC (Model, View, Controller) pattern.</em></p>
<p name="98c5" id="98c5" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">The best analogy by far that I’ve heard to explain the MVC pattern is the oft-repeated “Restaurant Analogy.” I didn’t want to repeat it again, so I sat there and tried to come up with an alternative analogy, but every analogy I came up with ultimately failed. Some sounded promising in the beginning, some I was even able to spin to some length, but in the end, none of them were able to explain MVC in as much detail as the restaurant analogy.</em></p>
<p name="9042" id="9042" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Seeing that I had no choice, I resigned myself to publishing yet </em><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">another</em></strong><em class="markup--em markup--p-em"> MVC restaurant blog post. I’ll try to add a detail or two here and there, and I hope to extend it somewhat to answer the second half of the question regarding separation of concerns.</em></p>
<figure name="0cc4" id="0cc4" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*IzQpW2ABs3920TjjaS-82Q.jpeg" data-width="628" data-height="371" src="/assets/images/posts/2017-06-07-2017-06-07_Welcome-to-the-MVC-Restaurant-0.jpeg"></figure><h3 name="f3ea" id="f3ea" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--figure">The Minimum Value Cafe</h3>
<p name="3d47" id="3d47" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">It’s been a long day; you’re hungry and grumpy, you just need a bite to eat before you go home. You slip into your favorite budget food spot, the somewhat crowded Minimum Value Cafe. Thankfully there’s an open table right there.</p>
<p name="b83c" id="b83c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">You sit down at the table and see the familiar menus. Some people like the one with the bright orange logo with a picture of a red panda hugging a bright-blue earth. Others prefer the menu with the red-yellow-green-blue ball logo; they say the waiters will serve you faster. There are others as well, though one thing everyone can agree on is to stay far away from the menu with the big blue “e” orbited by a gold ring, food ordered from there has been rumored to put a few too many innocent patrons down with nasty stomach bugs…</p>
<p name="1b11" id="1b11" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So you pick up your menu of choice. Some of these menus are getting pretty advanced; they open up to a page with your most commonly ordered dishes. As you are sitting and <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">browsing</strong> your menu, you are startled to hear a voice from behind “Hello, my name is Yechiel, and I will be your <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> for the night (the waiters here have their quirks), are you ready to place your order?”</p>
<p name="f6cd" id="f6cd" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">You point to your favorite tuna-and-egg sandwich, a double-shot espresso, and cheesecake for dessert. The waiter says “sure, it’ll take just a few milliseconds, can I get you anything to drink in the meantime?” and before you could respond, the waiter (err… <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong>) disappears in the back.</p>
<p name="d895" id="d895" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The back, far from the eyes of the common folks, is where most of the action happens. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> passes on your sandwich order to the sandwich boy, the coffee order to the barista, and the dessert order to the pastry chef (why these kitchen workers like calling themselves <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Models</strong> is anyone’s guess). The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Models</strong>, in turn, rush to the storage room to fetch the ingredients, get back to their stations to cook/prepare/assemble it all. When everything is ready, they hand it to the waiter (sorry, sorry, I know you like being called <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong>, it’s just hard to remember all the time, stop yelling at me like that!). The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> then assembles it all nicely on a big tray engraved with “The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">View</strong>” in cursive script, and brings it all out to you and sets it on your table.</p>
<p name="b7c1" id="b7c1" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">This whole process was so quick; the food was prepared in front of you before you even took your finger off the menu!</p>
<h3 name="d89d" id="d89d" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--p">So what’s it mean?</h3>
<p name="dc93" id="dc93" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">That’s the analogy in brief, and that’s what happens every time you browse the internet.</p>
<figure name="ee29" id="ee29" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*MQabORXtM4jpME-aH_2rYg.png" data-width="500" data-height="472" src="/assets/images/posts/2017-06-07-2017-06-07_Welcome-to-the-MVC-Restaurant-1.png"></figure><p name="63d4" id="63d4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">The process starts in your <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">browser</strong>, where you enter a URL, click on a link, submit a form, etc. That sends a request to the server where a <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> picks it up. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> handles the request, passing it on to the appropriate <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Models</strong>. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Models</strong> are where the main logic of the application takes place. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Models</strong> retrieve the data you need from the database(s) and assemble it based on the user’s request. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Model</strong> then hands that data to the <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> who, using templates found in the <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Views</strong>, assembles it into a format the user will enjoy. The <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Controller</strong> then takes the assembled <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">View</strong> and sends it back to the <strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">browser</strong> where the User gets entertained by endlessly repeating cat GIFs.</p>
<figure name="5354" id="5354" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*fQXprxQ0IpWtYQcJCROPmw.gif" data-width="480" data-height="360" src="/assets/images/posts/2017-06-07-2017-06-07_Welcome-to-the-MVC-Restaurant-2.gif"></figure><h3 name="d015" id="d015" class="graf graf--h3 graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--h3-strong">Separation of concerns</strong></h3>
<p name="839d" id="839d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3">Where this analogy really shines, in my opinion, is in answering the second part of the question: What is the benefit of separating each component from the others?</p>
<p name="9165" id="9165" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Let’s take a closer look at our restaurant. One can easily imagine a smaller establishment, where the chef is the one interacting with the customers, taking their orders, preparing them, and serving. Similarly, in smaller applications, you might be able to have all the logic needed in one file (that’s pretty much what happens in small static websites).</p>
<p name="902c" id="902c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Once the restaurant grows however and starts offering a variety of dishes, and the client base becomes too big for one person to handle, it becomes more and more important that each employee knows their place and job.</p>
<figure name="e269" id="e269" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p"><img class="graf-image" data-image-id="1*ubimVHPllTJ2KmkPrjFZAQ.gif" data-width="400" data-height="275" src="/assets/images/posts/2017-06-07-2017-06-07_Welcome-to-the-MVC-Restaurant-3.gif"></figure><p name="ed72" id="ed72" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">Imagine the chaos if the waiter decides to “help out” in the kitchen, no chef would ever accept such an intrusion. How about if the chef decided to busy himself with setting the tables, while orders kept piling up in the kitchen, customers would get annoyed real quick.</p>
<p name="ffe9" id="ffe9" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Most importantly is the issue of accountability. When everyone has a well-defined job, the manager knows where to go when something goes wrong. Is the food burnt? Speak to the chef. Are patrons waiting too long for their orders? See if the waiter needs help.</p>
<p name="6b5e" id="6b5e" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">In a well-oiled, well-defined system, every component has its job, and when things need fixing or tweaking, you know exactly where to go to fix them.</p>
<p name="bb7a" id="bb7a" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p graf--trailing">Similarly in your application. If every part of your code has its job and is in the right place, it makes it so much easier to catch and fix bugs, add features, and improve performance, without having to worry about stepping on the toes of any other component. After all, you never want to anger the ones who deal with your food ;)</p>
